Schwalbennester

  • 1 lb veal; cut in 4 thin slices
  • 1/2 tsp salt
  • 1/8 tsp sugar
  • 1/2 tsp white pepper
  • 1 tbsp Dijon-style mustard
  • 4 strips of bacon (one per slice of veal)
  • 4 hard-boiled eggs, peeled.  You're supposed to not over-boil them to avoid the yolk becoming gray, but I wouldn't worry too much about it.  I find boiling an egg for about 9 minutes is fine, but you can judge for yourself. Whatever the case, if any white stuff starts to spurt out of the eggs, your eggs are done, and at least one of the egg whites is now deformed. To easily peel after fully boiled, put the hot eggs in cold water.
  • 2 tbsp vegetable oil
  • 1 medium onion, diced
  • 3/4 c beef bouillon; heated. You normally buy beef bouillon in jars of cheap little dry cubes from the soup aisle in the grocery store.
  • 1 tbsp tomato paste
  • 2 tbsp unbleached flour
  • 1/4 c red wine

Dry veal on paper towels.  Roll it in a mixture of salt, sugar, white pepper, and mustard.  Place a bacon slice on top of each piece of veal.  Place a whole egg on top of each slice of bacon.  Rollup each slice of veal (jelly-roll fashion) and tie together with a string.  Heat oil in a frying pan and brown veal rolls well on all sides.  Add onion; sauté for 3 minutes.  Add the hot bouillon; cover and simmer gently 25 minutes.  Remove the veal from the pan.  Remove the strings from the veal and keep veal warm on a serving platter.  Add tomato paste to the pan drippings; stir.  Thoroughly mix flour and red wine to remove all lumps.  Add to sauce and cook until mixture thickens.  Add warm veal rolls and heat through.  Before serving, place veal rolls on a platter, pour sauce over the rolls and serve with pureed potatoes.  This dish is surprisingly easy to eat because you can cut through the egg and easily slice through the veal from the inside out.
