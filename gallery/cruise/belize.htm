Visiting Belize

 

The cruise ship couldn't dock at Belize's shore directly. It was too big. So here you see us zipping over to the shore in a "tender".  For those who know English well, you'd know this is a boat that takes you between the ship and the shore.

 

In Belize, our destination was the Xunantunich temple near the border of Guatemala, which was near a 2 hour drive each way. The temperature outside during our entire cruise and shore excursions ranged between 80 and 90F... extremely pleasant.  However, during this excursion, we drove away from the water, inland, and were stuck in a bus with air-conditioning that didn't work too well.  We were really hot in there, in otherwords. Maybe that's why I ended up taking a picture of two Coca Cola signs. Anyway, going inland, houses got smaller and more run-down. I suspect we'd see the same in the other countries as well. This street seems pretty well off though. Note any words you see in the picture would be in English, because Belize has chosen English as its primary language.

Finally we reach the Xunantunich temple. This temple was built by the Mayans. I forget the year, but I'm sure you can look it up elsewhere. Xunantunich translates to "stone lady", which is a name given to the temple by some locals that supposedly saw a lady float up the temple's side and disappear at the top. I, along with most of the other tourists, wondered what those locals were smoking. Just note that the name was not given to the temple by the Mayans who built it. So anyway, this is one of Belize's cherished treasures. Their appreciation came too little too late though. When they originally excavated the site, they sold off whatever they could for money, and there were a whole lot of looters too. Also one excavator even used dynamite to completely destroy the smaller structure which is to the right when facing the front of the temple, but blocked from your view by the structure that's directly in front of the temple, which you can see clearly here. I don't care if you didn't follow that. Just realize that if you face the temple, there's a hill to the right with destroyed ruins in it.

 

Here we reach the front of the temple. The stairs go up only part way, and the Mayans, according to the tour guide, climbed them on both hands and knees. When you reach the top of the steps, you enter the temple and continue climbing via a staircase inside it.

Here we return from the temple. To cross this river, we go on what's effectively a bridge. Let me describe this contraption. Two ropes act as guides for the bridge, and a crank on the left, behind the gray SUV, is umm.. cranked... manually, to push the bridge towards the other side of the water. Only one person was allowed in a vehicle while it was on the bridge. They allowed pedestrians on the bridge as well, but we had to stand in such a way that the platform was balanced. If we all stood on the front part, the bridge could not slide onto the far side. Why don't they just build a bridge, you might ask? Well, I asked this same question, and apparently they need to use this because the water level changes and they needed to compensate for that. Why can't they just build a higher bridge you might ask? I might guess it's because this is not a first-world nation, but maybe there's another reason that I'm not aware of.

 

I guess these guys call themselves Los Angeles as in "The Angels", not as in the Californian city. They provided music to entertain us while we ate authentic Belize cuisine consisting of rice and beans, chicken, fried plantain, and something that seemed like coleslaw, with mango juice to wash it all down. It was very good. Note the basket in front of them, obviously for tips. The music seemed very cheerful and it put me in a good mood, plus the guy in the middle smiled at me when I pointed my camera, so I gave them their first tip for the day. If you had dinner in this restaurant and did not tip them, shame on you.
