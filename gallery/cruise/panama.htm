Visiting Panama

 

Here we wear our life jackets and board our boats to travel across Gatun Lake.

 

And we're off. Gatun Lake has manitees, crocodiles, lots of fish, etc. We saw a snail kite flying nearby, and it took forever to find a sloth sleeping in a tree.

 

Can you find the sloth in this picture? Well, I'm sure I aimed the camera right, so keep looking.

 

As we leave our boats and remove our life jackets, the tribe greets us with some cheerful music.

 

The leader of the tribe tells us about his people and the area. He speaks only Spanish and some other ancient language, so the guy next to him in the white shirt translates for him. Mind you, that funky wooden hat of his was available at the souvenir shop for about $25, so it's easy to run your own tribe.

 

The girls of the tribe boogie down in a circle while the men play more cheerful music.

 

Yes, my camera stinks, so it's a little fuzzy. All these women are topless though, as you can see. They later pulled tourists onto the dance floor and dragged them around in a circle.

 

We kept hearing an annoying rooster. I found it hiding under one of their structures. I'm not exactly sure what you'd call it. Maybe a raised gazebo. In another part of the village, you can see the tribe's normal clothes hanging on a clothesline. They apparently only dress in their costumes for tourists. Quite a shock.

 

The children have this game where one of them hangs on the rope, then the other shakes the rope until they fly off it. Stupid kids. Well, anyway, they go to school in two shifts. By doing this, they always have kids available for tourists to see.

 

We went back onto Gatun Lake, going much faster this time, not stopping to observe the wildlife.

 

Some of the housing in Panama.

 

And we've reached the Gatun Locks of the Panama Canal.  There are 3 sets of locks, and the Gatun Locks are only one of them. The Miraflores Locks are another, and I forget the third. How these work is simple.  In the distance, you can see a green and white ship heading towards Singapore, carrying 5000 cars.  The two "locks" you see here will open, the ship will sail through, then the locks will close and the water will be drained until the ship is around 80 feet lower, then another pair of doors (another lock) opens and the ship sails free. There are tracks on either side of the canal. On these you can see little yellow vehicles, two on each, connected to cables which block the front and back of each ship. At first we thought perhaps it helps support the ship, but it does not. The tour guide thought it supported the ship's weight entirely. She's a moron. But the best explanation I heard was that the cables kept the ship moving along steadily so it didn't bump into the other ships.

 

This is a view of the water which will drain out as the ship is lowered. You can see the last of the Gatun Locks in the distance, and the tracks follow the canal downwards as well.

 

This is a view of the water fully drained out.  It's difficult to see, but the lock is opening here, and the ship is about to sail through.

 

Here's another view of that oversized ship. If you look closely, you can see the cables in front of it.

 

Ah, here's a very good picture of what I was talking about with the cables and tracks. The ship is happily heading out through the last of the Gatun Locks.  Probably not too happy, actually, since this ship paid a couple hundred thousand dollars to pass through. The Panamanians charge based on mass.

 

Here's a view of the main building/office complex/etc for the management of the Gatun Locks.
